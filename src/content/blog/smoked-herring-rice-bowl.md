# Smoked Herring Rice Bowl

**Prep:** 5 min | **Cook:** 10 min | **Total:** 15 min | **Serves:** 1

Canned smoked herring, rice, a fried egg, and something acidic to cut the richness. This is a $3 lunch that tastes like you planned it.

---

## Ingredients

- 1 can (3.5-4 oz) smoked herring fillets (in oil or water)
- 1 cup cooked rice (white, jasmine, or brown)
- 1 egg
- 1/4 small red onion, thinly sliced
- 1 tbsp rice vinegar or lime juice
- 1 tsp soy sauce
- 1/2 tsp sesame oil
- 1 tsp butter or cooking oil (for the egg)
- Pinch of red pepper flakes
- Everything bagel seasoning or sesame seeds (optional)
- Hot sauce (optional)

## Equipment

- Small skillet or nonstick pan
- [Rice cooker](https://www.amazon.com/dp/B007WQ9YNO?tag=cookfromwhat2-20) or small saucepan

---

## Instructions

**1. Quick-pickle the onion (5 min).** Slice the red onion thin. Toss it in a small bowl with the rice vinegar or lime juice and a pinch of salt. Let it sit while you do everything else. Five minutes is enough. The acid softens the raw bite and turns the onion pink.

**2. Warm the rice (2 min).** If using leftover rice, microwave it with a splash of water for 90 seconds. If cooking fresh, make it in a rice cooker or saucepan ahead of time.

**3. Fry the egg (3 min).** Heat butter or oil in a small skillet over medium heat. Crack the egg in. Cook until the white is set and the edges are crispy but the yolk is still runny. Season with salt and pepper.

**4. Assemble.** Put the rice in a bowl. Drain the herring and lay the fillets on top. Drizzle with soy sauce and sesame oil. Top with the pickled onion, the fried egg, and red pepper flakes. Add everything bagel seasoning or sesame seeds if you have them.

**5. Eat.** Break the yolk and mix it into the rice. The runny yolk acts as a sauce.

---

## Technique Tip: Why Canned Fish Belongs in Your Rotation

Canned fish is fully cooked, shelf-stable for years, and costs $2-4 per can. It is one of the highest-protein, lowest-effort foods you can keep in your pantry. Smoked herring, sardines, mackerel, and kippered snacks all work in this bowl. The smoking process gives the fish enough flavor to anchor a whole meal without seasoning it heavily. The reason most people skip canned fish is texture hesitation. Pairing it with contrasting textures (crispy egg edges, crunchy pickled onion, soft rice) makes it work. The acid from the pickled onion also cuts the richness of the fish oil so the bowl doesn't feel heavy.

---

## Substitutions

- **No smoked herring?** Use canned sardines, kippered snacks, or smoked trout. All work.
- **No rice?** This works on toast, over noodles, or with couscous.
- **No egg?** Skip it. The bowl still holds together with just the herring, rice, and pickled onion.
- **No rice vinegar?** Use lime juice, lemon juice, or apple cider vinegar for the quick pickle.
- **No sesame oil?** Leave it out. It adds depth but the bowl works without it.
- **Make it bigger:** Open a second can. Add sliced avocado. Add cucumber. This scales easily.

---

## Storage

This is a single-serving meal. Eat it fresh. The fried egg and pickled onion don't store well. If you meal prep, keep cooked rice and canned fish separate and assemble when ready to eat.
